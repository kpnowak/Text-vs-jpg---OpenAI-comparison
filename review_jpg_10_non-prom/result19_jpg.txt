### Review and Corrections Based on Non-Promotional Material Guidelines

#### Main Text and Headings Review

1. **Source Sentence:**
   "When you’re living with type 2 diabetes, it’s common to reevaluate and change your treatment to adjust to your changing personal health needs."

   **Guideline Source:**
   **Source - EFPIA Chapter 3, Article 17.ii**: "Informational or Educational Materials ... directly beneficial to the care of patients."

   **Deviations:**
   The source sentence could be interpreted ambiguously regarding what specific actions the patient should take, which could be perceived as promotional.

   **New Sentence:**
   "When managing type 2 diabetes, it’s important to regularly assess your treatment plan in collaboration with your healthcare provider to ensure it meets your changing health needs."

   **Reasoning:**
   This change ensures clarity and emphasizes collaboration with healthcare providers, aligning with non-promotional educational material guidelines.

2. **Source Sentence:**
   "It’s important that you feel empowered and ready to talk to your doctor whenever you feel a change could be beneficial."

   **Guideline Source:**
   **Source - Communicate With Care Pocket Guide, Article 2.i.**: "Maintain Professional Tone."

   **Deviations:**
   The original sentence lacks professional tone and can be perceived as overly reassuring.

   **New Sentence:**
   "It’s crucial to feel confident and prepared to discuss any potential treatment changes with your doctor when deemed necessary."

   **Reasoning:**
   This modification maintains professional tone and reduces any potential misinterpretation.

3. **Source Sentence:**
   "Start by educating yourself more about type 2 diabetes (like you’re doing right now!)—you’ll be able to communicate with your doctor more openly about treatment."

   **Guideline Source:**
   **Source - Communicate With Care Pocket Guide, Article 2.i.**: "Maintain Professional Tone."

   **Deviations:**
   The phrase "(like you’re doing right now!)" is casual and could be misinterpreted.

   **New Sentence:**
   "Start by educating yourself about type 2 diabetes to facilitate more open communication with your doctor regarding treatment."

   **Reasoning:**
   Ensures the text remains professional and clear, avoiding casual expressions.

4. **Source Sentence:**
   "Talk about your lifestyle and how a new treatment might be better suited for you."

   **Guideline Source:**
   **Source - EFPIA Chapter 3, Article 17.ii**: "Informational or Educational Materials ... directly beneficial to the care of patients."

   **Deviations:**
   The sentence might imply that a new treatment is inherently better, which could be perceived as an inducement.

   **New Sentence:**
   "Discuss your lifestyle with your doctor to evaluate whether a new treatment might be more appropriate for you."

   **Reasoning:**
   Emphasizes the need for a professional assessment without suggesting a new treatment is automatically superior.

#### Disclaimer and Information Review

6. **Source Sentence:**
   “The information on this website is intended for education purpose only and should not be substituted for medical advice from your doctor.”

   **Guideline Source:**
   **Source - Disclaimer Texts for Non-promotional Material, Article 3.i.**: “The image shown is a model and not a real patient."

   **Deviations:**
   The original sentence does not clearly specify the non-substitutive nature and educational purpose together.

   **New Sentence:**
   "The information on this website is intended solely for educational purposes and should not replace medical advice from your healthcare provider."

   **Reasoning:**
   Clarifies the material's educational purpose and non-substitutive nature more effectively.
